Funding Success in the Nonprofit Sector: A Data-Driven Analysis

Elena Elzinga, Johnny Lo, Audrey Davis

Abstract

Nonprofit organizations often struggle with inadequate funding despite being essential to society. While there is available information to help these nonprofits get funding, little work has been done to assess large-scale funding trends with nationally representative data. This study analyzed survey data collected from the Urban Institute, George Mason University, and American University, which included 4057 nonprofits from across the United States. Using weighted Rao-Scott adjusted chi-square tests and publicly available survey data, we explored what nonprofit organizational characteristics are associated with the likelihood of receiving funding after seeking it. Our results show that nonprofit size, sector, geographic service area, region, and government engagement all significantly predict funding success. Large nonprofit organizations, operating regionally within a state, with frequent government interaction, had the most success. These results suggest that, beyond mission alignment, structural and strategic initiatives strongly influence funding outcomes. Future research should explore causality through a longitudinal study and investigate if results differ in different countries.

Introduction

The Nonprofit Trends and Impacts Study is a survey created and distributed by the Urban Institute, American University, and George Mason University. The goal of this survey is to equip nonprofit leaders, policymakers, funders, and the public with insights to build sustainable and effective nonprofit organizations (Urban Institute, n.d.). The survey questions concern nonprofit programs and services, staff and volunteers, fundraising, donations, finances, and engagement with governments (Urban Institute, n.d.).  

Using the 2024 data in the Urban Institute Data Catalog, we analyzed multiple variables to help us answer the question, “What are the most important factors that influence a nonprofit’s ability to receive sustainable funding?”

Methods

Data Source

We used data from the Nonprofit Trends and Impacts Study, conducted by the Urban Institute in partnership with American University and George Mason University. This study is a multi-year panel that aims to provide insight beyond the financial data captured by IRS Form 990s. The survey provides nationally representative information on programs, services, staff/volunteers, donations, grants, and government funding.

Although the full study is multi-year, we limited our analysis to the most recent data collected between November 2023 and March 2024, which is focused on information related to nonprofits from January to December 2023. The specific dataset we used in the analysis was the YEAR-04-DATA-PUF.csv public-use file alongside the accompanying dd-nptrends-year-04-puf.xlsx codebook that explains the variables.  Both of these files are publicly available through their open data and resources page. The Urban Institute states that their data is de-identified to protect the privacy of respondents, with identifying variables (EINs, organization names, and addresses) removed. Continuous financial variables are modified with random noise (+/-3%) and “top-coded” (nonprofits over the 95th percentile recoded as the 95th percentile) to further reduce identification risk, while still keeping data in a usable form to run reliable statistics on it.

Data Collection and Representativeness

The original survey sampled 501(c)(3) public charities with annual expenses exceeding $50,000, excluding foundations, congregations, schools, and hospitals, due to their unique funding sources and operational characteristics.  Analysts used Employer Identification Numbers issued by the IRS to collect a national sampling frame of organizations stratified by organization size, subsector (NTEE classification), and state. They then randomly drew samples from this large sample frame and sent these nonprofits invitations to the survey. After data collection, the Urban Institute constructed survey weights to adjust for both sample design and differential response rates (like lower response rates from small organizations and nonprofits in certain sectors or regions). These adjustments allow us to treat the data as nationally representative of U.S. public charities with $50,000+ in annual expenses.

Details of new variables/metrics that we created

To analyze our data to answer our question, we created several new variables by consolidating or renaming the original binary answers in the dataset. Many adjustments were made, but some of the most helpful in answering our question are as follows: First, we generated a new variable (sector_group) to categorize nonprofits into ten major sub-sector groups using the NTEE classification codes (ntee1). Second, we compiled single columns from separate, but related, variables with multiple binary indicators. For example, from multiple columns related to service area (GeoAreas_Local, GeoAreas_State), we filtered the 1’s (yes) and renamed the 1 with the actual scope each organization reached (local, regional, state, etc.) and then combined the variables into a single labeled variable called GeoArea_Type. Third, we calculated funding success rates for 13 distinct public and private sources by pairing each “_Seek” and “_Rcv” variable. These created metrics allowed us to compare how often nonprofits that sought a particular type of funding succeeded in obtaining it, which we then compared across many different variables. Fourth, we created (CI_Low) and (CI_High) to calculate the confidence intervals that would then be placed onto the bar charts with the geom_errobar() function. Fifth, we created a (barwidth) variable to store the sample size of each category, which allowed us to vary the bar widths by putting the variable into the geom_col() function. This allowed us to see trends of nonprofits receiving funding, allowing us to more effectively answer our central research question: What main factors influence a nonprofit’s ability to receive government funding?

Statistical Methods

To test for associations between categorical variables relating to funding success, we used the survey package in R. The svydesign() function was used to create an object that accommodated our survey design. The svyby() and svymean() functions were used to calculate weighted proportions, and the svytable() function was used to obtain the sample size of each category, to vary the bar widths. Finally, we used the Rao-Scott adjusted chi-squared test, from the svychisq() function in R’s survey package. This method corrects the standard Pearson chi-squared test to account for the survey design and probability weights, which allows us to obtain more accurate p-values when data are unequally distributed. In particular, we tested whether funding receipt varied significantly by nonprofit subsector (sector_group), geographic area served (GeoArea_Type), organization size, and region (CensusRegion9). Because the dataset comes from a complex, stratified sample of U.S. nonprofits with varying response rates within organization size, region, and subsector, all statistical tests were weighted using the year4wt variable. This weight adjusts for nonresponse and underrepresentation to ensure that the results are nationally representative of the broader population of nonprofit charities with annual expenses over $50,000.

Results

  1. Nonprofit Size

A series of Rao-Scott adjusted chi-squared tests were conducted to examine whether nonprofit size (based on annual expenses) was associated with whether organizations that sought funding successfully received it. Results revealed statistically significant associations between nonprofit size and several funding outcomes. Significant relationships were observed for local government grants (p = .011), state government grants (p < .001), and federal government grants (p < .001). Additionally, receipt of local government contracts (p = .025), state government contracts (p < .001), and federal government contracts (p = .003) were all significantly associated with nonprofit size. Among private funding sources, significant associations were found for private grants (p < .001), community foundation grants (p = .006), donor-advised funds (p = .014), and corporate grants and donations (p = .013). However, no significant association was found for United Way funding (p = .111), combined federal funding (p = .062), or other funding sources (p = .373).

  1. Sector

Rao-Scott adjusted chi-squared tests were used to assess whether the nonprofit sector was associated with the likelihood of receiving funding among organizations that sought it. Significant associations were found for several funding sources. Receipts of local government grants (p = .007), state government grants (p < .001), and federal government grants (p = .0005), were all significantly associated with the nonprofit sector. Private foundation grants (p = .032) and community foundation grants (p = .017) also showed significant variation by sector, as did combined federal campaign funding (p = .003). However, no statistically significant associations were observed between the nonprofit sector and the receipt of local government contracts (p = .561), state government contracts (p = .186), or federal government contracts (p = .122). Likewise, no significant relationships emerged for donor-advised funds (p = .200), corporate grants and donations (p = .063), United Way funding (p = .179), or other funding sources (p = .325).

  1. Geographic Service Area

A series of Rao–Scott adjusted chi-square tests were conducted to assess whether nonprofits’ geographic service areas were significantly associated with their likelihood of receiving different types of funding. The results indicated significant associations for two types of funding: state government grants (p = .018), and federal government grants (p = .031).

No statistically significant associations were found for the remaining 11 funding types, including local government grants, local, state, and federal government contracts, and other private or federated funding sources such as private grants, community foundations, donor-advised funds, and corporate support (p  > 0.05; see table below).

  1. Census Region

Rao–Scott adjusted chi-square tests were conducted to examine whether Census Region was associated with the likelihood of nonprofits receiving various types of funding. Significant associations were found for state government grants (p = .001), federal government grants (p = .042), and local government grants (p = .040). These results suggest that the geographic location of a nonprofit, as measured by Census Region, may influence access to certain government funding opportunities. No significant associations (p > 0.05) were observed for other funding types, including government contracts, private or corporate grants, and federated giving programs such as United Way or Combined Federal Campaign funds.

  1. Strategic Government Interaction

A series of Rao–Scott adjusted chi-square tests were conducted to assess whether strategic government interaction was associated with nonprofits receiving various funding types. Significant associations were observed across most funding sources. Local government grants (p = .036), state government grants (p = .006), and federal government grants (p = .0002) all demonstrated significant variation by strategic government interaction. Additionally, government contracts at the local (p = .032), state (p = .021), and federal (p = .012) levels were also significantly associated with strategic interaction, as were private grants (p = .008), community foundation grants (p = .033), corporate grants and donations (p = .007), and other funding sources (p = .022).

In contrast, no significant associations were found for donor-advised funds, United Way funding, or combined federal funding (p > 0.05). These findings suggest that proactive or strategic engagement with government entities may play a key role in nonprofits’ success across a wide array of funding streams, particularly in obtaining public grants and contracts, as well as certain forms of private and institutional funding.

Discussion

Overview

This study examined which variables help in answering the question: “What impacts a nonprofit’s ability to receive funding?”. To answer this, we mainly looked within the scope of nonprofits that had success in receiving funds after seeking them out, and seeing if there were any notable or common trends. Additionally, this discussion mainly focuses on receiving government funding. The findings indicate that multiple factors, particularly nonprofit size, sector, geographic service area, and census region, include variables that significantly impact funding success. Some of these patterns are further supported by external research on nonprofits.  

1. Nonprofit Size

The size of the nonprofit was one of the most consistent predictors of funding success. Larger nonprofits demonstrated substantially higher success rates across all forms of government funding. For example, nonprofits with annual budgets exceeding $10 million had success rates above 92%, while small nonprofits under $100,000 reported success rates closer to 80%.

This pattern reflects the advantages larger organizations might have in grant-seeking: greater administration and staff, professionalized grant writing, and existing funder relationships. The Bridgespan Group’s analysis of "really big" nonprofits reinforces this size effect at even larger scales, showing that over 90% of nonprofits with $50M+ in revenue relied primarily on a single dominant funding stream, often government or philanthropy (Foster & Perreault, 2007). Our findings group together all nonprofits over $10M, but we still see higher success rates, which shows that nonprofits don’t necessarily need to fit into Bridgespan’s “really big” category of being over $50M+ to see increased success in funding. Instrumentl’s grant success data similarly highlight how resource capacity shapes outcomes, as smaller nonprofits may struggle with the complexity of funder requirements, especially at the federal level (Instrumentl Team, 2024). To see the relationship between size and individual forms of funding, as opposed to the overall success rate, click this link: https://jcliii.shinyapps.io/DAT_4500/

2. Sector

The sector the nonprofit was associated with also impacted its funding outcomes. Our results show that certain sectors have stronger success rates in receiving government funding, but that success isn’t uniform across all levels of government. For example, sectors like Health and Arts do very well with state and local grants, while Human Services and Public/Societal Benefit have better success federally. This probably reflects how different levels of government tend to prioritize different kinds of work. Federal agencies often focus on large-scale national programs, while state and local governments are usually more focused on supporting community-based services. These patterns line up with what Bridgespan sees at much larger organizational scales: certain sectors naturally fit with certain funding streams. What our data adds is that these sector-funder matches start to show up at smaller nonprofit sizes, and that the level of government matters as well as what specific sector the organization is affiliated with, when it comes to government grants. Other research adds another layer to this: even after nonprofits receive government funding, some sectors are much more financially dependent on it than others. For example, recent data from Candid and the Urban Institute show that over 70% of Human Services and Health nonprofits would fall into deficit without government support, compared to just over 50% for sectors like Environment or Religion. This reinforces that certain sectors aren’t just better positioned to secure government funding, but they’re also more reliant on it to continue their services. 3. Geographic Service Area

Across all three levels of government grants, nonprofits serving “Regional Within” areas (defined as organizations operating across multiple communities within a single state) consistently demonstrated higher success rates. This pattern may reflect a sweet spot where nonprofits can reach a larger population than just the local community, but still be small enough to really target the needs of the area and specialize their service to have the most impact on the community.  This might make them attractive candidates for state and local governments seeking a broad but targeted impact. Unlike purely local organizations, Regional Within nonprofits may have more resources, such as stronger infrastructure, professional grant writing capacity, and established partnerships across multiple jurisdictions, all of which may be more appealing to those who are in charge of distributing government grants. Conversely, national organizations, despite their scale, may be less aligned with local or state-level priorities, maybe cause lower success rates. These findings suggest that both the size of a nonprofit's service area and how closely it matches government funding priorities may significantly influence its chances of receiving grant funding. To explore the relationship between geographic service area and each of the thirteen types of funding, click this link: https://jcliii.shinyapps.io/Elena_App/

4. Census Region

We have found a statistically significant relationship between the regional distribution of NGOs and their receiving of funding, (p = 0.00055). While regions 1 through 5 (New England, Middle Atlantic, East North Central, West North Central, South Atlantic) appear to receive funding in a manner broadly consistent with expectations of the null hypothesis based on NGO distribution, regions 6 through 9 show substantial underrepresentation. This suggests potential regional disparities in access to or success in securing funding among nonprofits in these regions. These findings warrant further investigation into structural, policy, or capacity-related barriers faced by organizations in the East South Central, West South Central, Mountain, and Pacific regions (regions 6–9). Additionally, Census Region appears to have a significant relationship with local, state, and federal grants, but no other kind of funding. Regions 6-9 have a higher proportion of receiving government grants compared to its counterparts. To explore the relationship between region and each of the thirteen types of funding, click this link: https://jcliii.shinyapps.io/Audrey_Maps/

5. Strategic Government Interaction

Across all forms of funding, NGOs who met more frequently with government officials to discuss their work were better at receiving funding. This is because government officials often have influence, connections, and access to funding resources that can help NGOs thrive (Shefcik, 2025). The bar charts reveal that NGOs who “never” discuss their NGOs work with government officials receive funding at consistently lower rates than any other category. In contrast, NGOs who “Rarely”, “Occasionally”, or “Frequently” engage in this activity were more successful. Moreover, NGOs who met with government officials “All the time” are likely to have the strongest proportions. However, due to sampling size, that outcome is uncertain and yields the largest confidence interval. One reason this may happen is because larger NGOs often have the extra personal and bandwidth to create and maintain these essential relationships (Joseph, 2025). 7. Limitations

The present study is not without limitations. Firstly, the funding variables used to explore this research question are in the latter half of a 30-minute long survey and “we see respondents trickle off as the survey progresses” (H. Martin, Personal Communication, May 5th, 2025). While the data is weighted, there is a possibility for nonresponse bias, underrepresenting NGOs that with limited bandwidth to participate in the survey. Secondly, many of the variables asked questions using a Likert scale, which incorporates subjectivity into the study because one respondent's interpretation of “rarely” may differ from another. Furthermore, participants may over or understate aspects of their NGO due to social desirability bias, “the bias or tendency of individuals to present themselves in a manner that will be viewed favorably by others” (American Psychological Association, 2018). The third limitation concerns the lack of data overtime to perform a longitudinal study. Due to this, we can only make assumptions as opposed to inferring causality.
